Grit in the Cold: My Les Stroud moment at 21 was when I first saw “Survivorman.” Les Stroud came on screen—calm, deliberate, alone in the wild—and something inside me lit up. It wasn’t inspiration. It was a challenge. I thought, “If he can do it, so can I.”

So, naturally, I packed up the dumbest assortment of camping gear imaginable—gimmicky gadgets, canned food, an unrated sleeping bag—and trudged off alone into the snowy woods. The temperature was somewhere south of 20 below. I didn’t check. I was too busy romanticizing the misery. I had on blue jeans. Not snow pants. Not thermal layers. And steel-toe boots that would later try to claim a few of my toes.

I made it through that night by building a longfire nearly seven feet in length—just enough to match my body. I laid in my sleeping bag right beside it, stoking the flames between broken, shivering bouts of sleep. I kept the fire alive like my life depended on it—because it did.

By morning, my boots were scorched. My hoodie smelled like smoke. I was alive. And something inside me was harder, stronger, wiser.

Lesson Learned: Nature doesn’t negotiate. It exposes your weakness and respects your grit. Don’t rely on fantasy. Rely on fire. On will. On skill.

Shoutout: Les Stroud—true godfather of bushcraft. You lit the spark, brother. I kept it burning.
