🔥 Les Stroud Survival Story

If He Can Do It, So Can I

I was 21 when I first saw Les Stroud — calm, deliberate, alone in the wild — and something in me lit up. It wasn’t inspiration. It was a challenge.

So, naturally, I packed up the dumbest assortment of camping gear imaginable — gimmicky gadgets, canned food, an unrated sleeping bag — and trudged off alone into the snowy woods.

The temperature was somewhere south of 20 below. I didn’t check. I was too busy romanticizing the misery. I had on blue jeans. Not snow pants. Not thermal layers. And steel-toe boots that would later try to claim a few of my toes.

My brilliant plan also included bringing along a small bottle of alcohol. I figured it’d help me sleep or maybe boost morale. What it did instead was drop my body temperature even lower while giving me the illusion that I wasn’t a first-class nickel rookie.

By the time I started hiking, the snow had gotten deeper, and my boots were soaked through. I was miles from anything. No phone. No backup plan. Just frozen beans, dumb pride, and one very bad idea about to become a memory I'd carry for life.

I ended up digging into a snowbank and lighting what I now call the “Longfire” — a trench fire about six feet long that saved my dumb ass from frostbite or worse.

That night, I didn’t feel brave. I didn’t feel strong. But I proved something to myself.

And that’s the thing about grit. It doesn’t always look like the movies. Sometimes it looks like shivering in the dark, alone, embarrassed, and alive.
