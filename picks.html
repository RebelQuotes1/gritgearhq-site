📜 Grit in the Cold: My Les Stroud Moment at 21

I was 21 when I first saw Survivorman. Les Stroud came on screen—calm, deliberate, alone in the wild—and something inside me lit up. It wasn’t inspiration. It was a challenge. I thought, If he can do it, so can I.

So, naturally, I packed up the dumbest assortment of camping gear imaginable—gimmicky gadgets, canned food, an unrated sleeping bag—and trudged off alone into the snowy woods. The temperature was somewhere south of 20 below. I didn’t check. I was too busy romanticizing the misery.

I had on blue jeans. Not snow pants. Not thermals. Jeans. And steel-toe boots that would later try to claim a few of my toes. My brilliant plan also included bringing along a small bottle of alcohol. I figured it’d help me sleep or maybe boost morale. What it did instead was drop my body temperature even lower while giving me the illusion that I was warm. Classic rookie move.

I stayed the entire night, barely sleeping, curled up next to a 7-foot-long longfire I had to stoke constantly between waves of shivering and half-conscious dreams. That fire—my only warmth—probably saved my life. Without it, I would've frozen to death in my sleep.

By morning, I was a wreck. I stumbled back through the woods, barely coherent, fighting panic, numb from the waist down. I got lucky. Real lucky. I didn’t lose any toes, but it was close. My boots felt like coffins for my feet.

That night taught me more about survival than any TV show ever could. It wasn’t about heroism or being hardcore. It was about humility. And the very real, very stupid ways we underestimate nature.

If Les Stroud had been there, he probably would've smacked me upside the head with a pine branch and told me to go home. And he would’ve been right.

But I came out of those woods different—humbled, frostbitten, and one step closer to grit.

Shout out to Les for starting my journey.
