Grit in the Cold: My Les Stroud moment at 21 was when I first saw “Survivorman.” Les Stroud came on screen—calm, deliberate, alone in the wild—and something inside me lit up. It wasn’t inspiration. It was a challenge. I thought, “If he can do it, so can I.”

So, naturally, I packed up the dumbest assortment of camping gear imaginable—gimmicky gadgets, canned food, an unrated sleeping bag—and trudged off alone into the snowy woods. The temperature was somewhere south of 20 below. I didn’t check. I was too busy romanticizing the misery. I had on blue jeans. Not snow pants. Not thermal layers. And steel-toe boots that would later try to claim a few of my toes.

I made it through that night by building a longfire nearly seven feet in length—just enough to match my body. I laid in my sleeping bag right beside it, stoking the flames between broken, shivering bouts of sleep. I kept the fire alive like my life depended on it—because it did.

My steel-toe boots were soaked and freezing, so I took them off and set them near the fire to dry. I wrapped my bare feet in a hoodie and tucked them deep inside the sleeping bag. Then I used the old "buddy system" trick—putting my feet together so they’d warm each other. It worked, barely.

By morning, my boots were scorched from being too close to the fire. I had to smack out the live coals still clinging to them before I could slide them back onto my feet. It wasn’t comfortable, but it was that or frostbite.

There was no glory in it. No hero moment. Just stubbornness, instinct, and a little bit of bushcraft keeping me alive through one of the coldest, loneliest nights of my life.

The Lesson: Gear matters. Preparation matters more. But when it all goes sideways, it’s your willpower, your grit, and the tricks you remember that make the difference. The cold doesn't care about your ego. Nature doesn’t hand out second chances.

Shoutout to Les Stroud—the godfather of bushcraft—for planting the seed. I wouldn't recommend trying what I did. But I’ll always respect the fire it lit inside me.
