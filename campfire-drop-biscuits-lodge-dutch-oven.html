Campfire Drop Biscuits in a Lodge Dutch Oven

Campfire Drop Biscuits: The Full Field Report

First time out with a brand-new Lodge Dutch oven, straight from the box. No preseason layers, no oven cycles, no voodoo—just a thin wipe of olive oil, a small fire I could manage with one hand, and my daughter sitting next to me telling school stories while the crickets clocked in. I expected to weld dough to iron. Instead, I got biscuits that were crisp around the edges, dense in the middle, and exactly the kind of morale food that turns a plain evening into a memory. What follows is the long version—the why, the how, the things I’d change, and the tricks that actually matter when you’re cooking over coals instead of a dial that says 450°.

Why Do This the Hard Way?

Because the hard way teaches you what the easy way hides. I baby new gear for about five minutes, then I want truth. If a pot needs six seasonings and a pep talk before it’s useful, I don’t want it. Lodge cast iron is famous for being rugged, honest, and ready to work, even with that rougher factory texture that’s meant to hold its preseason. I wanted to see if that texture would actually cause sticking with a biscuit dough that’s heavy on butter. Answer: not when you run a thin oil film and bring the iron up to temperature with the fire. It released cleanly. No panic scraping. No ruined bottom crust. Just biscuits.

Fire, Pace, and Headspace

Here’s a thing I forget until I’m out there: the best part of campfire cooking is the pace. You don’t run it—the fire does. You adjust, you rotate the pot, you listen. With an oven, you set a temperature and the appliance handles the rest. With coals, you learn to read heat by sound and smell. You hear the faint hiss of butter, you smell flour browning, you feel the lid’s warmth on your knuckles. My daughter kept a running commentary about her day, and I kept one eye on the ember bed. We watched red turn to gray, fed a stick now and then, and let the iron take its time. If I had preheated the pot, I would’ve probably cut the bake in half. I wasn’t in a hurry. That was the point.

Exact Recipe I Used (Base)

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour
  • 1 Tbsp baking powder
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1 cup milk
  • 1 stick butter (cold)

Finish: Melt 4 Tbsp butter and pour over the top. Sprinkle with pink salt.

Method That Works Over Coals

  1. Mix dry ingredients first. Cold butter gets cut in with a fork or pastry cutter until the mix looks like coarse gravel.
  2. Add milk and use your hands. Don’t overwork it. This isn’t bread dough; it should be shaggy and a little sticky.
  3. Wipe the brand-new Dutch oven with a thin film of olive oil. Too much oil will fry the bottoms before the tops set.
  4. Coat a spoon with a bit of oil, scoop, and drop the dough. Give each biscuit room.
  5. Set the pot next to—not on top of—a bed of glowing red embers. Close the lid. Rotate the pot every few minutes so hot spots don’t bully one side.
  6. Cook until the tops firm up and the bottoms show color; depending on your heat, this can echo the 16–20 minute oven range, or go longer if you skipped preheat like I did.
  7. Finish with melted butter and a pinch of pink salt. Let the biscuits rest two or three minutes so the crumb sets and the butter gloss settles.

What I’d Change Next Time

Preheat, maybe. If you want speed and a bit more lift, preheating the pot is the move. I like the slower ramp for flavor and crust, but if you’re feeding a crowd or racing daylight, get the iron hot first. Top heat. You don’t need a lidded oven covered in coals to bake, but it can even the heat and speed things along. If your lid is flat, toss a few coals up there. If not, no stress—just rotate more. Mix-ins. This base welcomes flavor: chives, green onion, jalapeños, shredded cheddar, tuna packets, bacon bits—choose your own adventure. “Dense” is the style here; it eats like food, not air.

Fire Management: The Real Skill

You don’t manage temperature as a number; you manage heat shape. Coals heat steadily; flames spike. I start by building a teepee, let it collapse into a coal bed, then feed small sticks around the perimeter so I have fresh coals rolling in without scorching blasts. If a side is running hot, rotate the pot, or lift it for a minute with a small branch under the bail to bleed off heat. You don’t need expensive gear for this—long tongs and decent gloves are enough. Move coals to fix problems, not the entire oven. It’s safer and gives you better control.

The Brand-New Iron Question

Every cast iron thread online eventually turns into a seasoning debate. Here’s my lived take from this run: a factory-fresh Lodge with a rougher surface can still bake clean if you do two things—thin oil and patient heat-up. The roughness is there to hold seasoning, not to ruin your night. Wipe, warm, and trust the process. After this session, the oil and butter baked in and the pot looked better than when I opened the box. That’s the quiet truth of cast iron—every cook is a tiny upgrade.

Add-Ins and Variations

  • Green + sharp: chives and sharp cheddar.
  • Heat: thin jalapeño rounds or a whisper of cayenne.
  • Protein trail mix: tuna, chopped summer sausage, or bacon crumbles.
  • Sweet camp treat: cinnamon sugar in the dough, honey butter on top.
  • Herb route: rosemary and black pepper with a parmesan finish.

These biscuits are dense on purpose. They won’t puff like a laminated, folded dough. They’re meant to stick to your ribs and carry you through a cold night or an early morning hike. Think of them as edible morale patches.

Troubleshooting That Actually Solves Problems

Bottom too dark? You parked the pot on live flame. Slide it to the side, set it on a bed of glowing coals, and rotate. A small stick under the bail lifts the oven enough to calm the heat if your ground is too hot.

Raw center? Resist the urge to add more flame. You need more time, not a blowtorch. Add fresh embers and keep the lid closed. Patience sets the crumb.

Sticking? Two likely culprits: heavy oil pool or too-cold iron. Wipe a thin film, then warm the pot with the fire so the surface is ready before dough hits it.

What to Serve With Them

These biscuits are perfect beside something. Grilled steak, pan stew, venison chili, or just a jar of jam and a knife if it’s morning. I’ve eaten them solo, too—two biscuits and coffee will keep you productive until lunch. If you need to reheat, wrap in foil and park near the coals for a few minutes. The butter finish helps them revive like they were just baked.

Storage and Camp Reality

If you’re not eating them all (rare), let them cool and stash in a zip bag. They’ll hold overnight in cool weather. At home, they’ll be fine a day or two on the counter. Reheat in a skillet with a scant dab of butter or on a baking sheet at medium heat. I don’t overcomplicate storage in the field—these are best when they meet air, butter, and hands in the same five minutes.

FAQ (From Friends Who Saw the Video)

“Do you have to preheat a Dutch oven for biscuits?” No. It speeds things up and can add lift, but coming up to temp with the coals works. Rotate more and be patient.

“Won’t new Lodge stick?” If your oil is thin and your heat-up is gradual, it shouldn’t. Mine didn’t.

“What size oven did you use?” A classic family-sized Lodge. Use what you have; just don’t crowd the dough.

“Can I bake directly on coals?” Yes, if you know your fire. I prefer beside an ember bed for a gentler bake.

Field Notes (From This Exact Cook)

  • The first five minutes tell you everything: the smell of butter, the soft hiss, the lid’s heat. If it’s roaring, you’re too hot.
  • Thin oil is king. You’re seasoning as you cook, not deep-frying.
  • Rotate like a clock: quarter turns every few minutes.
  • Let the butter finish sit. The shine isn’t just pretty—it locks in moisture.
