Grit in the Cold: My Les Stroud Moment

When I heard the forecast call it a "cord cold," that was my cue. Most folks take that as a warning. I took it as a dare.

I talked two knucklehead friends into joining me for a winter survival run. The plan? Head into the woods, build a massive lean-to, slap some mylar blankets up behind us, and bask in the radiant warmth of a roaring fire like three kings of the cold. It sounded solid in theory. On paper we were practically experts. In reality, we were flirting with frostbite.

The wind was brutal. The lean-to rattled like a cheap kite. Our fingers stopped working the way fingers are supposed to work. Water bottles froze solid. I could barely get a lighter to spark. One of the guys, the first to break — hit his limit and bailed back to the car before dark. The other stuck it out: a rich kid with a high-end setup. Real snow boots, thermal pants, a decent bag, and even a sleeping pad to keep the ground from leeching his body heat. I was running on stubbornness and false confidence.

We both laid out on either side of a long fire we had to keep alive all night, taking turns feeding it like it was our only lifeline because it was. I tried to dry my boots too close to the flames, and they started to melt. I trembled uncontrollably in and out of sleep, curled up in a half-shiver, half-delirium, wondering if this was the night I finally crossed the line from tough to stupid.

The only reason I didn’t end up in the obits for hypothermia is because my buddy took pity. He gave me his sleeping pad and leaned back against his own pack like a half-frozen Buddha in snow pants.

The next morning, we were wrecked. Our bones ached, our feet were numb, and our spirits were about one degree above dead. We trudged through a foot of snow half a mile to the trail, two more to the car. Didn’t talk much. Didn’t need to. We’d both survived something. Not epic. Not heroic. Just… real.

Cold has a way of stripping the fantasy off survival. It’s not about inspiration. It’s about figuring out what kind of man you are when there’s no one coming to save you.

And that weekend, I figured out I had more grit than gear — and just enough of both to make it home.
