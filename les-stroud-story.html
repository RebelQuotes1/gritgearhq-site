So, naturally, I packed up the dumbest assortment of camping gear imaginable — gimmicky gadgets, canned food, an unrated sleeping bag — and trudged off alone into the snowy woods.

The temperature was somewhere south of 20 below. I didn’t check. I was too busy romanticizing the misery.

I had on blue jeans. Not snow pants. Not thermal layers. And steel-toe boots that would later try to claim a few of my toes.

I talked two knucklehead friends into going with me.

One wussed out almost immediately. The other let me sleep on his pad while he leaned against his backpack.

We shivered all night beside a 7-foot longfire. In the morning, we hiked back — no glory, no trophies. Just a cracked ego and a newfound respect for hypothermia.

Lesson learned: Nature doesn’t care about your pride or your poor planning. The wilderness is humbling — and it doesn’t hand out second chances.

Shoutout to Wes, the friend with gear and a heart, for keeping my stubborn ass alive that night. And thank you, Les Stroud — you didn’t just spark something in me… You lit the fire I nearly froze beside.
