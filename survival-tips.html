Survival Tips (That Actually Help When It’s Not a TV Show)

This isn’t a doom scroll. It’s a calm, useful walk through the things that keep people safe: mindset, shelter, fire, water, food, navigation, first aid, and a little campcraft. You can practice every skill in your backyard this weekend, preferably with coffee and someone you like talking to.

1) Mindset Beats Gear (But Gear Helps)

Survival starts in your head. Not with a titanium shovel that doubles as a satellite dish. The calm person who can slow their breathing, make a short list, and do the next smallest thing wins. That’s the whole game. I keep a simple loop for anything weird: Pause → Look → Decide → Do → Review. It keeps panic from stealing your intelligence.

Practice drill: once a week, kill your house breakers to one room for an hour. Phones away. Eat, read, or hang out with a headlamp. You’ll instantly see what’s missing and what’s fine.

Know the difference between comfort and need. You can be uncomfortable and perfectly safe. You can be comfy and in danger if you’re wandering toward a cliff in soft socks or unarmed hammock camping in bear country. The mindset piece is giving yourself a short plan and sticking to it. Write it down if your brain is buzzing.

2) Shelter: Stop the Wind, Steal Back Heat

You lose heat three ways outdoors: conduction (ground steals it), convection (wind steals it), radiation (space steals it). Your job is to block those thieves. A tarp, some cordage, and a ground pad will outperform fancy jackets when the sky gets rowdy.

Fast tarps you can set up under stress

  • Lean-to: wind at your back, tarp staked low in front, reflect heat from a small fire.
  • A-frame: line between two trees, tarp over the top, low pitch for stormy nights.
  • Plow point: one corner high on a line, three corners staked tight—great in wind.
Cordage pro move: pre-tie four guy lines with loops on one end and taut-line hitches on the other. Stow them attached to your tarp so you’re not playing knot sudoku in the rain.

If you’re on the ground, insulate under you first. A pile of leaves under your pad is free R-value. If you’re on a cot, block wind underneath—cots are heat thieves in a breeze. In cold rain, pitch your tarp low enough that you can reach the line from your knees. It’s not glamorous, but it works when you’re tired.

3) Fire: Heat, Morale, and a Way to Dry Socks

Fire buys you warmth, water treatment, cooked food, dry gear, smoke for bugs, and a morale bump that’s hard to measure but easy to feel. Practice in decent weather so you’re ready in garbage weather.

My three-layer plan

  1. Spark: Bic lighter or ferro rod. Have both. Lighters are lazy. Rods ignore wind.
  2. Tinder: dry shavings, cotton + petroleum jelly, or SurvivorCord’s tinder strand in a pinch.
  3. Structure: small, dry pencil-thin sticks first. Only add wrist-thick wood after you’ve got a coal base.
Wet wood trick: split down to the dry heart with a knife and baton. Feather the dry inner faces. Feed constantly until the steam stops lying to you.

Build a small fire you can keep honest. Big fires are for photos. Cook on coals, not drama. When you’re done, drown, stir, and feel for heat with the back of your hand. That patch of woods will be there next weekend because you didn’t turn it into a news story.

4) Water: Treat, Carry, Stretch

Most adults run better at two to three liters per day when working. In hot weather, more. I carry one bottle on me and a dirty water bag in the pack. Treat with a squeeze filter, boil in a pot, or use tablets if you’re moving fast and the water tastes like a swimming pool anyway.

  • Find it: look for green belts, low ground, animal trails, and where topographic lines pinch tight (ravines).
  • Pre-filter: a bandana or coffee filter saves your real filter from clogging with pond salad.
  • Stretch it: sip, don’t chug; cool your core with shade and wind; keep salt and calories coming so water doesn’t just run through you.
Boil math: a Dutch oven or covered pot comes to boil faster than an open cup. Lids are little miracles.

5) Food: Simple Fuel That Tastes Like a Plan

I pack the same template: a salty snack, a quick carb, a simple protein, and a hot thing at night. Jerky + tortillas + instant potatoes + whatever meat or beans I have equals a real meal. If I’ve got the Dutch oven, drop biscuits or bannock turn morale up to eleven.

Camp bread baseline

2 cups flour, 1 Tbsp baking powder, 1 tsp salt, 1 cup milk, 1 stick butter. Mix dry, cut in butter, add milk, drop into a lightly oiled pot. Bake over coals, rotate, butter the tops, pink salt. That’s it.

6) Navigation: Don’t Be Lost, Be “Temporarily Geographically Confused”

Phones die. Apps update at the worst time. Bring a laminated paper map in a gallon bag, a baseplate compass, and two brain cells set aside for cardinal directions. If you never used a compass, here’s the ten-second version: turn the housing so north lines up with the needle, keep the little shed pointed at north, and walk the arrow. That’s enough to keep you honest between landmarks.

Dead-simple breadcrumbs

  • Take a photo of every junction and weird tree. That’s a time-stamped breadcrumb.
  • Count paces between features (every two steps is one pace). 60 paces ≈ ~100 feet for lots of adults. Your stride will vary—good enough.
  • Stop walking when you’re unsure. Doubt plus motion equals lost. Sit, snack, plan, then move.

7) First Aid: Fix What Kills First

You don’t need to be a medic. You do need to know how to stop bleeding, open an airway, recognize heat/cold injuries, and improvise a splint good enough to walk on. My kit is boring on purpose: gloves, pressure bandage, gauze, tape, triangular bandage, meds (ibuprofen, antihistamine), a tourniquet I know how to use, and a few blister fixes.

Heat & cold: if someone is shivering and clumsy, get them dry and feed them warm, salty carbs. If they’re hot and stupid, shade + water + electrolytes + rest. Both problems get better slowly; that’s normal.

8) Kits & EDC: Layers You Can Explain Without Apologizing

Build layers so you’re never far from being fine. Here’s my stack: pockets → small waist pack → day pack → duffel in the truck. Each layer repeats the big three (fire, water, shelter) but gets fancier as it grows.

Pocket level

  • Lighter + mini ferro rod, folded bandana, small flashlight or headlamp, a couple of water tabs.

Waist pack

  • Filter, soft bottle, compact tarp, 25–50 ft of SurvivorCord, snacks, simple first aid.

Day pack

  • Extra warm layer, gloves, hat, fixed blade knife, small saw, bigger first aid, real map, compass, rain shell, power bank.

Duffel in the truck

  • Wool blanket, old boots, water jug, duct tape, jumper cables, more snacks than you think, and a way to cook if stranded.

9) Signals & Comms: Be Findable

Cell service is a coin toss in big woods. A signal mirror weighs nothing and throws a bright “I’m here” farther than you’d expect. Whistles beat yelling. If you play in remote places, a PLB or satellite messenger turns epics back into stories you tell over biscuits.

  • Three blasts on a whistle is the universal “help” pattern.
  • Bright cloth on a branch reads better from the air than dark jackets in dark trees.
  • At night, a steady light is “I’m here,” a slow triple flash is “I need help.”

10) Bring the Family: Small Jobs, Real Fun

Kids are great at survival practice if you make it a treasure hunt. Give them a job they can finish—a pile of thumb-thick sticks, a knot to master, or setting the table rock. Praise the result, not the chaos, and remember they’ll remember the hot chocolate more than your sermon on wind angles.

Kid-safe tasks

  • Collect dry leaves and pencil sticks.
  • Fill clean water bottles from a safe source you point to.
  • Learn the bowline and the taut-line hitch with a shoelace challenge.

Partner sanity pact

  • Agree on a turn-around time. No mountain is worth an argument.
  • Share a map photo to both phones.
  • Snack before anyone gets hangry. Works better than therapy sometimes.

11) Urban & At-Home: The Power Goes Out, Not Your Brain

Most “emergencies” are boring: lights out, roads iced, water shut for a day, or you’re stuck waiting on a tow. A small bin next to the pantry turns those into “movie nights with lanterns.” Load it with headlamps, batteries, a crank radio, stove fuel, instant meals, and a deck of cards.

  • Keep one big pot (cast iron or steel) strictly for non-electric cooking. Gas stove, camp stove, or fire pit—it all works.
  • Freeze a couple of water jugs. They keep food cold and become drinking water as they thaw.
  • If you run a generator, run extension cords like you love your house. Dry, shaded, and away from puddles. You know why.

12) Pocket Checklists (Rip & Stick in Your Kit)

S.T.O.P. Card

  • Stop. Breathe. Drink water.
  • Think. What actually matters in the next hour?
  • Observe. Weather, daylight, injuries, map, water.
  • Plan. One small step. Then the next.

10-Minute Camp Setup

  1. Pick high, dry ground with wind at your back.
  2. Tarp up first. Bags stay dry while you futz with everything else.
  3. Collect pencil-to-thumb sticks, stage them by size.
  4. Water sourced and treated or set to boil.
  5. Warm layer on before you get chilled.

Simple Food Pack (per adult, per day)

  • 2,500–3,000 calories mixed salt/sweet
  • At least 2 liters of water + treatment
  • One solid hot meal you look forward to (stew, ramen + add-ins, or biscuit night)
